Overview

Hinshaw Mortgage Group (a fictional mortgage company) has 24 loan officers operating across five branches, each with different market conditions, borrower types, and production expectations. Because of that, comparing loan officer performance isn’t as simple as looking at who closes the most loans or who has the best close rate. Every metric tells only part of the story, and relying on one of them creates a misleading picture of who’s actually performing well.

To solve this problem, I built the "Loan Officer Performance Score" metric. This metric works by evaluating both componenets of the loan officers job - generating production while maintaining pipeline efficiency. The score rewards the things that matter most—closing loans and driving volume—but it also accounts for the operational behaviors that directly impact profitability, like fallout, suspense, and extensions. Extensions cost the company money and often signal borrower frustration or weak pipeline management, so a true top performer shouldn’t just bring borrowers in the door—they should be able to convert them consistently.

With the score in place, the dashboard makes it easy to compare loan officers by plotting their Performance Score against their fallout and extension behavior. This naturally creates four quadrants that reflect real patterns in how loan officers work. Top Performers score high and keep fallout low—they close a lot of loans and move borrowers through the process efficiently. Volatile officers also produce volume, but their higher fallout or extension usage shows inconsistency in pipeline management. Underperformers maintain clean pipelines but struggle to generate meaningful production. And High‑Risk officers fall short on both sides, with low production and high fallout or extension rates. Because the quadrant lines are based on the team’s median performance score and median fallout rate, the chart adjusts to the actual distribution of the group, giving leaders a clear, honest view of where each loan officer stands.

By combining production metrics with pipeline efficiency, the dashboard turns performance into something measurable and comparable. It helps leaders quickly identify top performers, uncover hidden inefficiencies, and make informed decisions that strengthen both the borrower experience and overall profitability.

Key Insights
  • Daniel Patel holds the highest Performance Score at 100.00. His 385 closed loans, 76.23% close rate, and 33% extension rate make him the most balanced and efficient producer.
  • Charlotte leads all branches in average Performance Score. Charlotte loan officers consistently score above the team median, reflecting strong conversion and disciplined pipeline management.
  • Dallas posts the lowest branch Performance Score. Elevated fallout and extension rates across Dallas loan officers pull the branch to the bottom of the distribution.
  • Sophia Shaw closes the most loans (477) but carries a 39.97% extension rate. She contributes 8.36% of total closed volume but also 8.30% of company‑wide extension cost, showing her efficiency does not scale with her production.
  • Emily Turner has the highest extension percentage at 40.15%. This significantly increases operational cost and reduces her overall Performance Score despite strong production.
  • Top Performers combine high volume with low fallout. Loan officers in this quadrant average close rates above 75% and fallout rates below 6%.
  • Volatile performers show strong production but elevated fallout or extensions. Many exceed 350 closed loans but carry fallout rates above 21% or extension rates above 35%.
  • Underperformers maintain clean pipelines but lack production scale. These loan officers typically close fewer than 260 loans annually despite low fallout.
  • High‑Risk loan officers fall below the median on both production and efficiency. They combine low volume (<250 loans) with high fallout (>20%) or high extension usage (>35%).
  • Sophia Hernandez has the lowest Performance Score at 72.34. Her 68.36% close rate, elevated suspense rate, and 24.64% fallout rate are the primary drivers of her low score.
  • Branch leaders highlight market‑specific strengths. Top performers include Emily Turner (Atlanta), Harper Johnson (Charlotte), Mia Hernandez (Chicago), Isabella Garcia (Dallas), and Daniel Patel (Los Angeles).
  • Extensions remain a major cost driver across the team. Loan officers with extension rates above 35% contribute disproportionately to the average $1,618 extension cost per loan.
